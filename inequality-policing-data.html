POLICE BRUTALITY IN THE US

Data visualization pertaining to racial inequalities in policing

INTRODUCTION

After a video went viral of police using excessive force that caused the death of George Floyd in May 2020, the issue of police brutality was brought to the forefront of American life as protests emerged nationwide for months afterwards. Racially-biased policing in the US, however, has been problematic since the inception of our police force.
Our anaylsis of data from some of the largest police departments in the U.S. depicts the racial disparity that exists today. We have made use of a comprehensive dataset and litigal work centered around the issue of police brutality. We hope to present an objective, factual visualization of marginalized groups' unjust pattern of treatment by the police in the U.S.


ABOUT THE DATA

The data presented is taken from mappingpoliceviolence.org and was processed into csv format and categorized by a data scientist, John Miller. Miller published a robust, 3-part dataset that contains pertinent information on police violence over the last decade. Our group downloaded particular sets to create graphic representations from and only scraped the surface of the available resources.

To what extent are marginilized communities disproportionally targeted by police?

In order to first understand the extent to which marginilized communities are disproportionally targeted, a dataset containing fatal interactions with police was evaluated. Causes of death from this dataset capture all aspects of interactions with police, not only those where a police officer directly killed a person. For example, the dataset includes cases with a vehicular cause of death, as would occur in a fatal police car pursuit. Given the racial bias that is evident in traffic stops, this dataset provides an all-encompassing view of police interactions, not only those where police were the direct cause.
By breaking down these fatal interactions proportionally by race, it can be seen European-American/ White people account for a little less than half of the total cases (roughly 47%). However, by accounting for racial breakdown within the United States we can visualize how minorities communities are disproportionally affected. The racial divide for the United States is as follows: White alone (76.3%), Hispanic or Latino alone (18.5%), Black or African American alone (13.4%), Asian/Pacific Islander (6.1%), and Native American/ Alaskan (1.3%).
After adjusting the number of fatal interactions based on the US racial divide, it is clear to see the racial-bias against minority communities, as they form an extremely disproportionate number of cases.

THE STOP-AND-FRISK

Terry Vs. Ohio

Background

The "stop-and-frisk", or the "Terry stop", is a highly disputed policing practice. The latter name comes from a landmark case, Terry v. Ohio (1968), in which the U.S. Supreme Court addressed the compatibility of this tactic with the Fourth Amendment's ban on "unreasonable searches and seizures." The Court upheld this tactic's constitutionality, so long as it was supported by "specific and articulable facts" that justified criminality and danger. While "articulable facts" should diminish racially biased stops, racial prejudice in this practice is still observed in data from police departments nationwide.
Today, all police agencies are required to record their stops. The collection of stop-and-frisk data is not standardized nationally, however, as a survey showed that 23 out of 44 major PDs don't require specific data collection. The limitations in this kind of data from police departments is daunting and poses challenges since the collection is not uniform. For example, Los Angeles and Oakland PD do not consistently record the race of the suspect. Even in NYC, who is considered the national leader in this kind of data collection, a federal monitor’s report to the court found that in 25% of stops in 2015, officers failed to properly document the suspicion that prompted the stop.

The Fourth Amendment:

    "The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated, and no Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause, supported by Oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the place to be searched, and the persons or things to be seized."

New York City PD Terry Stops

In Floyd v. City of New York (2013), Judge Scheindlin relied on data from benchmark studies to determine whether blacks and Hispanics were stopped disproportionately in relation to racial composition, crime rates and various other factors. It was even found the NYPD carried out more stops in areas with predominantly black and Hispanic populations. Along with evidence from the NYPD that officers were acting with discriminatory intent, Judge Scheindlin's findings led her to rule that the NYPD's practices were violating the 14th Amendment and ordered a series of reforms to their policing practices.
The monthly calendar to the right depicts data from NYPD on their stop-and-frisk practices. The number of people stopped that were tagged as BLACK consistently contributes 50-60% of the month's stops. Compare this to how approximately 25% of NYC's population is Black / African American.

Background

This correlation matrix is designed to show the lack of conformity of police policies. Click on the buttons below for a further expliantion of what each term means. Inspiration for this plot was taken from curator of this dataset.

Any use of force by a member of this department shall be documented promptly, completely and accurately in an appropriate report, depending on the nature of the incident. The officer should articulate the factors perceived and why he/she believed the use of force was reasonable under the circumstances.
A police officer is under a duty to intercede and prevent fellow officers from subjecting a citizen to excessive force, and may be held liable for his failure to do so if he observes the use of force and has sufficient time to act to prevent.

Police officers shall use firearms with a high degree of restraint. Officers' use of firearms, therefore, shall never be considered a routine and is permissible only in defense of life or to prevent serious bodily injury to the officer or others - and then only after all alternative means have been exhausted.

Firearms shall not be discharged at a moving vehicle unless a person in the vehicle is immediately threatening the officer or another person with deadly force by means other than the vehicle.

When feasible, members shall identify themselves as a police officer and issue a warning before discharging a firearm.

Police Officers are prohibited from utilizing the Lateral Vascular Neck Restraint (LVNR), choke hold, neck hold, and/or any other restraint that restricts free movement of the neck or head.

Law enforcement officers should use only the amount of force necessary to mitigate an incident, make an arrest, or protect themselves or others from harm. The levels, or continuum, of force police use include basic verbal and physical restraint, less-lethal force, and lethal force.
De-escalation tactics and techniques are actions used by officers, when safe and feasible without compromising law enforcement priorities, that seek to minimize the likelihood of the need to use force during an incident and increase the likelihood of voluntary compliance.
