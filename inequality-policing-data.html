POLICE BRUTALITY IN THE US

Data visualization pertaining to racial inequalities in policing

INTRODUCTION

After a video went viral of police using excessive force that caused the death of George Floyd in May 2020, the issue of police brutality was brought to the forefront of American life as protests emerged nationwide for months afterwards. Racially-biased policing in the US, however, has been problematic since the inception of our police force.
Our anaylsis of data from some of the largest police departments in the U.S. depicts the racial disparity that exists today. We have made use of a comprehensive dataset and litigal work centered around the issue of police brutality. We hope to present an objective, factual visualization of marginalized groups' unjust pattern of treatment by the police in the U.S.


ABOUT THE DATA

The data presented is taken from mappingpoliceviolence.org and was processed into csv format and categorized by a data scientist, John Miller. Miller published a robust, 3-part dataset that contains pertinent information on police violence over the years 2011-2018. Our group downloaded particular sets to create graphic representations from and only scraped the surface of the available resources.

Terminology

A case where a person dies as a result of being shot, beaten, restrained, intentionally hit by a police vehicle, pepper sprayed, tasered, or otherwise harmed by police officers, whether on-duty or off-duty.

Unarmed:

  • - not holding any objects or weapons when killed
  • - holding household/personal items that were not used to attack others (cellphone, video game controller, cane, etc.)
  • - holding a toy weapon (BB gun, pellet gun, air rifle, toy sword)
  • - an innocent bystander or hostage killed by a police shooting or other police use of force
  • - a person or motorist killed after being intentionally hit by a police car or as a result of hitting police stop sticks during a pursuit

THE STOP AND FRISK

Terry Vs. Ohio

The user was asked to open the Spotify web application, and try to adjust the size of the navigation bars on both the right and left-hand sides of the screen. The user hovered over the vertical border of the left-side navigation and was able to resize the menu. For the right-hand side "Friend Activity" menu, the user attempted to resize the bar in the same manner, but was unsuccessful. She was also unable to minimize the bar completely.

Task 2

As a second task, the user was asked to find a Spotify playlist that she "liked." (Note: In Spotify, users can only "like" playlists that they are not the owners of, so the user was being asked to find a playlist that she did not originally create.) On the home screen, the user verified that there is does not exist a way to be channeled to a page that displays her save playlists. Upon realizing playlists are only listed on the left-side nav bar, the user scrolled through the list under the "Playlist" heading. The user clicked through a few playlists before finding one of the correct type.

The above sequence models track the actions that the participant took in response to directions given to her. This initial evaluation of how the user interacted with the current Spotify interface comprised the first part of this project's contextual inquiry (CI). The behavior exhibited informed the design thinking to make improvements to the user experiene with my lo-fidelity prototye.
