A MEXICAN HORROR STORY

november 2019

I recant my most chilling experience at a Mexican restaurant.

By daniela torres

I was sweating for all the wrong reasons.

I hoped my first bite of this “Mexican” food would cause that oh-so-familiar perspiration on my forehead-- the kind that comes with piping hot food and spices doing the hokey-pokey on my tongue. My friends at the table continued on laughing in light-hearted banter over our entrees: “Last meal as freshmen!” Meanwhile, I broke a cold sweat as I realized that I was going to have to pay for this overpriced, horrendously under-spiced, Mexican dish.

Just the name Holy Frijoles had struck blaring alarm bells throughout my core. Hailing from San Antonio, Texas, I was skeptical of the Mexican food scene in Baltimore. So, my instincts kicked into high gear when my friends suggested we try this place out. Something in my gut was urging me to speak up. I liken it to a sixth sense, trained by my hometown’s culture and yearly visits to my Mexican grandmother’s home, that guides me to tasty, authentic Mexican food. Sometimes, I feel a little version of my Abuelita appear on my shoulder with her glasses on, ready to inspect the food in front of us.

However, it was my friends’ plan to go out to dinner for our last meal together as freshmen. I did not want to be the obnoxious Texan who thinks she’s too good for the local selection of restaurants. So I quietly succumbed and found myself faced with a standard Tex-Mex dish in front of me: two chicken fajita tacos with a small side of rice and beans. The rice was a deceptive orange color, tricking me into believing that a flavorful experience awaited. I popped a spoonful into my mouth and was immediately assailed by rubber turf. A cruel rice imposter snuck itself onto my plate and whispered thousands of insults to me. I tried to cover my Abuelita’s ears.

Taken aback, I had to move onto the chicken tacos. I asked Abuelita to say a few Ave Marias for me: we both knew I needed some divine intervention to get through the meal. I found the attempt at seasoning the chicken equivalent to a six-year-old shooting a half-court shot-- weak and laughable. Its preparation was like going to Vegas when you’re under 21, or thinking that tomorrow is Friday and realizing it’s only Tuesday-- completely and utterly unimpressive. I then had maybe two bites of the beans before I decided that I cared too much for both my physical and mental wellbeing to eat anymore, and pushed the plate away.

Instead of vibrant food bursting with spices-- what Mexican food should be-- Holy Frijoles serves food fresh out of a freezer prepared with the least possible effort needed to feign Mexican-style-flavors. As we finished our meals, I swore to my Abuelita to never return. I asked my friend from the area what she thought about the food. “I didn’t think it was that bad,” she said. I felt Abuelita shudder.

About this post

In the 2019 fall semester, I took a class on food and culture blogging. Our very first assignment was a short piece about the worst food we've had to eat. Stories in my class ranged from childhood memories of their least favorite dish, to fine dining gone wrong, to foreign delicacies that didn't go down well. Our professor pushed us to use humor and a lot of metaphors to convey our visceral experiences.
